The Changing Character of National Risk in the Twenty-First Century

Veritatem Quaerere

"Modern strategic risk no longer emerges from a single source. It arises from the interaction of political, economic, technological, environmental, and societal systems whose complexity exceeds traditional models of analysis."

National security has traditionally been understood through the lens of military capability, territorial defence, and state-to-state conflict. While these remain important considerations, the strategic environment of the twenty-first century has become considerably more complex. Governments, businesses, and civil society now operate within a world of deeply interconnected systems, where events in one domain can rapidly generate consequences across many others.

The global financial crisis, the COVID-19 pandemic, cyberattacks on critical infrastructure, disruptions to international supply chains, and the accelerating pace of technological innovation have demonstrated that modern strategic risk frequently develops across multiple sectors simultaneously. The boundaries between domestic and international security, public and private responsibilities, and physical and digital environments have become increasingly blurred.

This report argues that effective strategic risk assessment requires an integrated and multidisciplinary approach. Analysts must consider not only immediate threats but also the structural conditions that create vulnerability, including economic dependence, institutional resilience, information integrity, technological change, and geopolitical competition.

The report identifies five broad trends shaping the contemporary risk environment:

  • Intensifying geopolitical competition between major powers.
  • Rapid technological change, particularly in artificial intelligence, cyber capabilities, biotechnology, and quantum computing.
  • Increasing economic interdependence alongside systemic vulnerability.
  • The growing influence of information operations on public trust and democratic institutions.
  • The interaction of climate, energy, and critical infrastructure risks.

Rather than treating these developments as isolated challenges, this report proposes that they should be understood as mutually reinforcing components of a wider strategic system. Effective decision-making therefore depends upon continuous horizon scanning, structured analytical methods, and an institutional culture that values evidence over assumption.

The purpose of this publication is not to predict individual events. Strategic foresight cannot eliminate uncertainty, nor can it anticipate every future development. Instead, the objective is to provide a framework for disciplined analysis that enables organisations to identify emerging risks earlier, evaluate their potential consequences more effectively, and strengthen resilience before crises occur.

Ultimately, strategic advantage belongs not only to those with superior resources, but to those capable of understanding complexity, recognising change, and adapting before others do.
